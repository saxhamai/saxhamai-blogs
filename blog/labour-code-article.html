New Labour Code: Better Social Security or Just a Salary Reshuffle?

By Nitin Pawar • June 5, 2026 • 8 min read

A Question Millions of Employees Are Asking.

Founder-led insight on corporate compliance, employee psychology, and operational restructuring in Indian businesses.

When India's New Labour Codes were introduced, they were presented as one of the most significant labour reforms in decades. The objective sounded straightforward: strengthen social security, improve retirement benefits, simplify compliance, and create a more standardized wage structure across industries.

On paper, these reforms appeared to be a win for employees.

Yet, many working professionals have found themselves asking a simple question after receiving revised salary structures:

"If my salary increased, why is my take-home pay almost the same?"

This question sits at the center of the debate around the New Labour Code.

Understanding the Reform

India consolidated 29 complex labour laws into four major labour codes:

  • 🔹Code on Wages
  • 🔹Industrial Relations Code
  • 🔹Code on Social Security
  • 🔹Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code

One of the most discussed provisions is the wage definition framework, which effectively requires that wages (including Basic Pay) constitute at least 50% of total remuneration.

Historically, many companies kept Basic Pay relatively low and distributed compensation through various allowances such as Special Allowance, Conveyance Allowance, Flexible Benefits, and other reimbursements. This structure helped reduce statutory obligations such as Provident Fund (PF) and Gratuity.

The new framework changes that equation entirely.

The 50% Rule: The Core of the Debate

Under the revised framework, Basic Pay becomes a larger component of salary. Allowances are restricted in proportion to total pay. Because of this, PF calculations are performed on a higher wage base, and Gratuity calculations increase. Long-term retirement benefits improve.

📊 Example: ₹10 Lakh CTC Impact

For an employee earning ₹10 lakh annually:

• Basic Pay increases significantly.
• PF & Gratuity contributions increase.
• Monthly take-home salary reduces by 2-5%.

The total compensation remains unchanged. Only the distribution changes. This is where the debate begins.

What Employees Expected vs. What They Are Experiencing

Most employees interpret a salary revision in a straightforward way: Higher salary should mean higher take-home pay, more disposable income, and better ability to manage household expenses.

For someone paying home loan EMIs, rent, school fees, and insurance premiums, the amount credited into the bank account each month is often the most important number. When an employee receives an appraisal but sees only a marginal increase in take-home salary, disappointment is natural.

The Actual Experience: Across industries, employees report a similar pattern. CTC remains the same or increases slightly, but Special Allowances disappear. Monthly take-home pay changes very little.

From an employee's perspective, it feels as though the same money has simply been moved from one column to another.

"It feels like a salary reshuffle rather than a salary increase."

Whether that perception is fair or not, it reflects a genuine concern that organizations and policymakers cannot ignore.

The Company's Perspective

To understand the issue fairly, it is important to examine the employer's side as well. As discussed in our systems analysis, compliance structures force behavioral shifts.

Most companies are not restructuring salaries because they want employees to earn less. They are restructuring because they must comply with evolving labour regulations.

Organizations commonly:

  • Increase Basic Pay and adjust HRA.
  • Remove or reduce Special Allowance.
  • Recalculate PF liabilities.
  • Increase Gratuity provisions.

From a compliance perspective, these changes are necessary. From a financial perspective, they often increase the employer's statutory obligations. In many cases, the company's total cost remains similar while the salary structure changes significantly.

The Hidden Benefit: Long-Term Wealth Creation

The strongest argument in favor of the New Labour Code is long-term financial security. A higher Basic Salary translates to a Higher Provident Fund Balance. Both employee and employer contributions increase. Over a 20-30 year career, this can create a significantly larger retirement corpus.

It also means Higher Gratuity payouts after long-term service, as gratuity is directly linked to wages. The reforms aim to strengthen formal social security mechanisms rather than relying solely on monthly cash compensation. From this perspective, the reform encourages long-term financial discipline.

Are Both Sides Right?

Surprisingly, yes. The conflict exists because each stakeholder evaluates success differently.

👨‍💼 Employees

Are right because monthly cash flow matters. Immediate financial obligations cannot be postponed. A salary increase that barely affects take-home pay feels disappointing.

🏛️ Policymakers

Are right because retirement security matters. Higher PF and gratuity improve long-term financial stability. Standardized wage structures reduce loopholes.

🏢 Employers

Are right because they must comply with regulations. Salary restructuring is driven by legal requirements and systemic enforcement rather than corporate preference.

The Communication Gap & The Bigger Question

Perhaps the biggest challenge is not the labour code itself. The biggest challenge is communication. Many employees receive revised compensation structures without understanding why their components changed or how the revised structure benefits them in the future.

Without transparency, employees naturally assume that companies are merely rearranging salary components without providing meaningful benefits. This misunderstanding creates frustration and distrust.

The real question is: Can labour reforms improve social security without making employees feel that their salary increase has disappeared?

This is where transparency becomes critical. Employees deserve clear explanations regarding expected take-home changes. When employees understand the "why" behind the numbers, resistance decreases and trust improves.

🔚 Conclusion

The New Labour Code is neither purely a social security revolution nor merely a salary reshuffle. It is both.

For employees focused on immediate financial needs, the reforms may feel frustrating because higher deductions reduce visible salary growth. For employees focused on long-term wealth creation, the reforms provide stronger retirement protection and greater financial security.

The ultimate success of these reforms will not be measured solely by compliance statistics or revised salary structures.

It will be measured by whether employees genuinely understand how these changes affect their present income and future financial well-being.

Because in the end, employee welfare is not only about creating benefits. It is also about ensuring that employees clearly understand them.
